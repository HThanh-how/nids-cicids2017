\begin{abstract}
Network intrusion detection is a cornerstone of modern cyber-defense, yet
the transition from an accurate offline classifier to a scalable,
continuously serving system is frequently overlooked. In this paper we
present a reproducible comparative study of classical machine learning and
deep learning models for network intrusion detection on the widely used
CICIDS2017 benchmark. We design a unified pipeline covering data cleaning,
feature selection, and class-imbalance handling, and we evaluate Logistic
Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost, and a multilayer perceptron under a
single protocol using accuracy, precision, recall, macro-F1, and ROC-AUC.
Beyond predictive quality, we quantify training and inference cost so that
model selection reflects deployability rather than accuracy alone. Finally,
we propose a cloud-native deployment design that packages the selected
model as a containerized microservice with an automated CI/CD pipeline.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Enterprise and telecommunication networks are exposed to an ever-growing
volume and diversity of attacks, ranging from volumetric denial-of-service
campaigns to stealthy infiltration attempts. The scale and velocity of
modern traffic make purely manual analysis infeasible and motivate
automated, learning-based intrusion detection systems (IDS) that can flag
malicious flows in near real time~\cite{buczak2016survey}. Over the past
decade, machine learning (ML) and, more recently, deep learning (DL)
methods have become the dominant approach to this problem.

Despite substantial progress, two practical gaps persist. First, many
studies report high accuracy on a single model and dataset configuration,
making fair comparison across model families difficult because
preprocessing, feature selection, and evaluation protocols differ from one
work to another. Second, the literature emphasizes offline predictive
quality while paying limited attention to \emph{deployability}: the
computational cost of training and inference, and the engineering path from
a trained artifact to a scalable, continuously operated service. In an
operational setting, a marginally more accurate model that is an order of
magnitude more expensive to serve may be the inferior choice.

\paragraph{Contributions.} This paper addresses these gaps with the
following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A reproducible preprocessing and evaluation pipeline for the
        CICIDS2017 dataset, released openly to support replication.
  \item A unified comparison of classical ML (Logistic Regression, Random
        Forest, XGBoost) and a deep learning model, evaluated jointly on
        accuracy, precision, recall, macro-F1, and ROC-AUC, together with
        training and inference cost.
  \item A cloud-native deployment design that serves the selected model as
        a containerized microservice driven by an automated CI/CD pipeline.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
Machine learning and data mining techniques have been applied extensively
to intrusion detection, spanning supervised classifiers, unsupervised
anomaly detectors, and hybrid schemes; the survey of Buczak and
Guven~\cite{buczak2016survey} provides a broad overview of methods and the
trade-offs between detection accuracy, interpretability, and computational
cost.

Benchmark datasets have strongly shaped progress in the field. The earlier
KDD'99 corpus and its refined variant NSL-KDD~\cite{tavallaee2009nslkdd}
exposed and partially corrected problems such as redundant records and
unrealistic class balance, but both remain dated with respect to modern
traffic and attack types. UNSW-NB15~\cite{moustafa2015unswnb15} introduced
a more contemporary feature set and attack taxonomy. CICIDS2017
~\cite{sharafaldin2018cicids} further improved realism by capturing several
days of labeled traffic that mixes benign behavior with a range of current
attack families, which is why we adopt it as our primary benchmark.

While many works report strong results on these datasets, comparatively few
combine (i) a fair, single-protocol comparison across model families with
(ii) an explicit treatment of computational cost and a concrete deployment
path. This paper targets precisely that combination.

\section{Dataset and Preprocessing}
\label{sec:data}
The CICIDS2017 dataset~\cite{sharafaldin2018cicids} contains roughly five
days of labeled network flows, comprising benign traffic together with
several attack families such as denial-of-service, distributed
denial-of-service, brute-force, web attacks, and infiltration. Each flow is
described by more than eighty statistical features derived from packet and
flow-level measurements. The data are distributed as multiple CSV files,
which we merge into a single corpus for processing.

\subsection{Cleaning and Encoding}
We first normalize column names by stripping leading and trailing
whitespace, then replace infinite values with missing markers and drop
records containing missing values, together with exact duplicates that
would otherwise bias evaluation. We cast the task as binary classification,
encoding every non-benign flow as the positive (attack) class and benign
flows as the negative class. Numeric features are standardized to zero mean
and unit variance using statistics estimated on the training split only, to
avoid information leakage.

\subsection{Feature Selection}
To reduce dimensionality and inference cost, we rank features by importance
using a Random Forest~\cite{breiman2001random} trained on the training
split and retain the top-$k$ features
(TODO: report the chosen $k$ and the selected feature list). This keeps the
most informative attributes while discarding redundant or low-signal
columns.

\subsection{Class Imbalance}
Benign flows substantially outnumber attack flows, which can bias a
classifier toward the majority class. We mitigate this on the training
split using the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique
(SMOTE)~\cite{chawla2002smote}; the test split is left untouched so that
reported metrics reflect the original distribution.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
\subsection{Models}
We compare four models that span a representative range of capacity and
cost. \emph{Logistic Regression} serves as a linear baseline. \emph{Random
Forest}~\cite{breiman2001random} is a strong bagging ensemble of decision
trees. \emph{XGBoost}~\cite{chen2016xgboost} is a gradient-boosted tree
method that is widely competitive on tabular data. Finally, a
\emph{multilayer perceptron} (MLP) represents the deep learning family with
two hidden layers.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
All experiments are conducted on the free tier of Google Colab using
scikit-learn and XGBoost. We use a stratified train/test split and, for
model selection, stratified $k$-fold cross-validation on the training
split. A fixed random seed is used throughout to ensure reproducibility,
and the complete pipeline is provided in the accompanying notebook.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Let $TP$, $TN$, $FP$, and $FN$ denote true positives, true negatives,
false positives, and false negatives. We report
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP},\qquad
\mathrm{Recall}=\frac{TP}{TP+FN},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{F1}=2\cdot\frac{\mathrm{Precision}\cdot\mathrm{Recall}}
{\mathrm{Precision}+\mathrm{Recall}},
\end{equation}
together with overall accuracy and the macro-averaged F1, which weights the
two classes equally and is therefore informative under class imbalance. We
additionally report the area under the ROC curve (ROC-AUC). To capture
deployability, we measure wall-clock training time and average inference
time per sample for each model.

\section{Cloud-Native Deployment Design}
\label{sec:deploy}
To close the gap between an offline classifier and an operational detector,
we package the selected model as a stateless REST microservice. The trained
artifact is exported and wrapped in a lightweight inference service
(implemented with FastAPI), containerized with Docker, and deployed on
Kubernetes, where horizontal replicas provide scalability and resilience.
A CI/CD pipeline automates the build, test, and deployment stages so that a
new model version can be validated and rolled out without manual
intervention; this mirrors the continuous-delivery practice expected of
cloud-native systems and is reproduced for the paper artifact itself, whose
PDF is compiled by a hosted CI runner.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We presented a reproducible, cost-aware comparison of machine learning and
deep learning models for network intrusion detection on CICIDS2017, paired
with a cloud-native deployment design. Our results
(Tables~\ref{tab:results}--\ref{tab:cost}) indicate that
\textbf{TODO (model)} provides the best balance of detection quality and
serving cost, reinforcing the view that deployability should be a
first-class criterion in IDS model selection.

Several directions extend this work. Online and continual learning would
allow the detector to adapt to concept drift as traffic and attack patterns
evolve. Explainable detection would help analysts triage alerts and build
trust in automated decisions. Graph-based and sequence models could exploit
relational and temporal structure beyond per-flow features, and a federated
formulation would enable collaborative detection across distributed
telecommunication nodes without centralizing sensitive data. These
constitute the intended trajectory of our continuing doctoral research.
